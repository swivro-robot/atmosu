Capturing dead or inoperable satellites in orbit

If a satellite is in orbit and isn't functional, it should be removed.

This spacecraft, MISAT, maneuvering using hypergolic fuels, nitrogen ACS thrusters, and reaction wheels, is capable of precisely approaching and capturing small to medium sized satellites, to then bring them to a larger ship (such as SpaceX's starship) to be de-orbited and burned up while re-entering Earth's atmosphere, or, to bring back to Earth for repairs, or disassembly. When MISAT is active, it extends its solar panels so that it can keep its batteries charged while electricity is being used. The advanced automatic-adjusting computer software running on the computer in this spacecraft, as well as the arms which open and close using only electric motors, both draw power from these batteries, so it's important to have solar panels to recharge.

It's important to note that MISAT is not an expendable spacecraft. It will not be launched into orbit to be used once and then disposed of. MISAT is fully capable of refilling its propellant tanks in orbit via a nearby MISAT refilling station and recharging itself via the extendable solar panels. The only time the operation of a specific MISAT would need to be halted is if said MISAT is damaged in some way. In that case, a servicing vehicle with special equipment will undock from a nearby refilling station to visit the damaged MISAT and perform all repairs that it can perform. If the MISAT is damaged beyond repair in space, it will be brought back to Earth where humans will repair it. If it's not worth repairing that specific MISAT, it will be de-orbited and will burn up in Earth's atmosphere. Re-usability in space is important these days. Expendable spacecraft and launch vehicles are a thing of the past.
