GALAXY-WIDE INTERNET

Gbit enables galaxy-wide communication and internet using optical lasers

Currently, there's no internet on the Moon, on the beautiful planet of Mars, which is where future civilization will live, and in interplanetary space in general. Gbit would change this. It would enable anyone, anywhere in the solar system to access the internet to stream videos, download files, view websites, message friends, etc. Depending on your location in space, the connection may or may not be delayed, but this is to be expected with communication, especially over the internet, across such a large distance. The only way to avoid this would be to create some sort of communication method that greatly exceeds the speed of light (or perhaps send radio signals through a wormhole), but we can't do that right now, and we aren't certain that it's even physically possible. Gbit is your best bet for internet in deep space. Though it may be delayed at great distances as mentioned, delayed internet that is still accessible is better than no internet at all. The internet is what connects you to the entire world, and it's important to humanity.

Gbit satellites are powered by a giant fusion reactor that floats in space. The core of this fusion reactor exceeds 25 million degrees Fahrenheit, and it's the loudest and brightest thing you could ever imagine. This fusion reactor is called the Sun. Gbit satellites are solar powered. They receive energy from the Sun (and/or other stars) with their two large deployable solar panels. Gbit satellites also feature a microwave transmitter to transmit energy to dying Gbit satellites, but we'll talk more about that later. Gbit uses tiltable mirrors to send signals precisely using its infrared optical laser. Gbit has the capability of sending information via radio signals, but the infrared laser would be the primary method of transmitting information throughout the Gbit satellite network.

MORE DATA SENT FASTER, COMPARED TO TRADITIONAL RADIO WAVES

In contrast to radio waves, the infrared light used for laser communications packs lots of data/information into smaller waves, allowing ground stations other Gbit satellites, or other types of satellites, to receive more information simultaneously. Thus, more data can be delivered in a single downlink even though the infrared light used in laser communications aren't actually "faster" in terms of velocity. This is one of the primary reasons Gbit uses infrared lasers for communication over traditional radio waves.

EVERY CITY ON THE MOON WOULD HAVE EASILY-ACCESSIBLE INTERNET

COMMUNICATIONS WAY BEYOND OUR SMALL SOLAR SYSTEM

The Gbit network can expand beyond our solar system, so that humans can communicate with each other and can contact Earth even when beyond the orbit of Pluto, the planet farthest away from our Sun. One single Gbit satellite can send a message to another star system without the aid of any other Gbit satellites, but a constellation of Gbit satellites will be used for redundancy and reliability. However, Gbit, currently, is still limited by the speed of light (c) as it uses infrared light to transmit information, so we can't use Gbit in any meaningful way across an infinite distance or an extremely long one (intergalactic).

GBIT WOULD BE MASS PRODUCED EVERY MONTH, EVERY YEAR

GBIT is designed to be as simple as possible. As Elon Musk, SpaceX Founder, CEO, and Chief Engineer says, the best part is no part. The less parts you have, the less that can potentially go wrong, and the less complicated the spacecraft is. Gbit would be tested numerous times and would be as simple as possible before being used. This minimizes the chances of a failure occurring in all Gbit satellites, making the Gbit satellite network extremely reliable.

GBIT DOES NOT AFFECT LIVING HUMAN BEINGS IN ANY WAY AT ALL

Your smartphone emits infrared light all the time, but you don't notice it or see it, because the naked human eye cannot see infrared light. Your television remotes, your toaster for heating up bread in the morning, and electric heaters, all use infrared radiation. So, unless you're intentionally standing directly right in front of the infrared laser (who would do that?), you'll be fine. Because Gbit satellites also have the capability of sending/receiving radio signals, we'd also like to inform you that you're sending & receiving radio signals every second via your internet router, smartphone, laptop, etc. Gbit will not harm you or anyone else in any way.
